Looking around in
2045: Mumbai

CONCEPT NOTE

As one of the forecasters, Peter Stamp envisions the future and shares: “The subway—if there is still one—will know when you want to enter. It will analyze your face and optimize the speed. If you are elderly, it will allow more time for the door to open than if you are young. If you are, let's say, overweight, it will offer you a broader seat. It will be clear what you want to do after you leave. For example, if you exit at the Natural History Museum in London, it will offer you tickets for that or anything else you might want there, right where you exited the subway.”

I was captivated by his discussion on how large AI models could be used to make sense of transportation networks. Beyond the physical adjustments and ergonomic enhancements, the deployment of AI and surveillance technology also raises questions about privacy, safety, data breaches, and other unforeseen consequences. I chose to visualize a sort of hacked, messy version of Peter's scenario through the Mumbai locals, and through this scenario, we aim to explore these issues.

About the artist

Ayaz Basrai is co-founder of The Busride Design Studio, a leading architecture and interior design firm in India. Busrai graduated in industrial design, specialising in product design, from the National Institute of Design, Ahmedabad, in 2003. More recently, Busrai heads The Busride Lab in Goa, working with speculative fiction, heritage conservation, and their ongoing India Futures Project, trying to visualise and birth meaningful Indian futures.
